## The question

AI governance discussions often begin with a framework and work backward. I
wanted to understand the machinery underneath the framework: which assumptions
about capability, adoption, growth, power, and verification make a proposed
control necessary—or plausible—in the first place.

The result is a structured reading and production program. Its argumentative
chain is **resources → capabilities → automated research → growth and power →
governance response → verifiable controls**. A disagreement upstream should
change the conclusions downstream, rather than disappearing inside a generic
category called “AI risk.”

## How it works

Each unit is organized around a governing question and ends with a practical
artifact:

- an assumption and indicator register for scenario forecasts;
- a metric-and-control map for compute and scaling claims;
- an audit of takeoff-model assumptions;
- economic and distributional risk registers;
- a compute-assurance architecture;
- a short institutional control memo.

The curriculum is deliberately not a certification checklist. Readings are
treated as claims with mechanisms, evidence, and possible falsifiers. Sources
and policy versions are scheduled for periodic revalidation.

## Governance lesson

Controls make more sense when their dependency chain remains visible. If a
proposal relies on assumptions about model scaling, task duration, chip supply,
or institutional observability, those assumptions should be explicit enough to
monitor and revise.

## Limits

This is a learning program, not an authoritative framework. It is incomplete,
reflects my current judgment, and will change as I finish the readings and test
the artifacts. Publishing it is a commitment to make that development legible,
not a claim that the field has been mastered.
